WHAT IS PUBLIC INTERNATIONAL LAW?
Public international law governs the relationships between independent States. An
independent State, is a sovereign political entity that has a defined territory, a
permanent population, a form of government, and the capacity to enter in to
international relations.
Subjects of international law include independent States, intergovernmental
organizations, international non-governmental organizations, and individuals.

INTERNATIONAL TREATIES AND CONVENTIONS
An international treaty or convention is an international contract that creates law for
consenting States. A State could be party to a treaty by direct negotiation or via
subsequent accession, or ratification of its provisions.
CUSTOMARY INTERNATIONAL LAW
Customary international law is derived of State practice, supported by a measure of
opinion iuris, or a subjective acceptance of State practice as law.
• State practice has to be consistent, uniform, and regular.
• In order to be free of a developing rule of customary international law, a State has
to be a persistent objector, there by negating opinion iuris.
GENERAL PRINCIPLES OF INTERNATIONAL
LAW
General principles of public international law are derived of general principals in
national State laws that dictate how States behave within the international
community.
• What constitutes a civilized nation, as opposed to an uncivilized nation is a matter
of subjective opinion, and debate. Eurocentricity has often been interpreted as
deciding this argument, albeit inconclusively.
JUDICIAL DECISIONS AND THE WRITINGS OF
THE QUALIFIED PUBLICISTS
Although Article 38 (1) (d), and Article 59 formally excludes a doctrine of stare
decisis in public international law, the decisions of the International Court of Justice,
and the writings of the qualified publicists have effect as binding precedent when
subsequently interpreted as reference, and source material.
SOVEREIGNTY IN INTERNATIONAL LAW
According to the declarative theory of sovereignty, a State becomes sovereign when it satisfies the
criteria for Statehood, as detailed in the Montevideo Convention 1933:
• A defined territory;
• A permanent population;
• A form of government; and
• The capacity to enter in to international relations.
Note however, that Statehood cannot be obtained by the use of force.
According to the constitutive theory of sovereignty, a State only becomes sovereign, when its
sovereignty is recognized by other sovereign, and independent States.
SELF DETERMINATION IN INTERNATIONAL LAW
Internal self determination, is the right of a people to constitute a sovereign
legislature, to decide what their laws are to be; to constitute an executive arm of
government, to implement these laws; and to constitute a fair, and impartial judiciary
to interpret the same. It is the right of a people to decide their own political destiny.
External self determination, is the right of a people to constitute a new, sovereign,
and independent nation State; either to create a new State, or to secede from and
existing State.
• Unlike a right to internal self determination, the international community is careful
to recognize a right to external self determination, as once recognized, it is
absolute.
JURISDICTION IN INTERNATIONAL LAW
Jurisdiction in public international law entails both the jurisdiction to prescribe, and the jurisdiction to enforce. For example, the
British parliament could legislate to ban smoking in the streets of Paris, but this will not be enforceable in France.
Uncontroversial bases of jurisdiction
• Territorial jurisdiction: A State has jurisdiction over events within its territory;
• Nationality principle: A State has jurisdiction over her nationals;
• Protective jurisdiction: A State has jurisdiction to protect her national interests.
Controversial bases of jurisdiction
• The effects doctrine as an extension of territorial jurisdiction: To exercise jurisdiction over events having an effect within
national territory;
• The passive personality principle: To exercise jurisdiction over events affecting State nationals;
• Universal jurisdiction: To exercise universality in international law.
IMMUNITY IN INTERNATIONAL LAW
Immunity in public international law extends the notion of the sovereign immunity of
States to its heads of State, government officials, and other public officers acting in
a capacity of iure imperii or for, and on behalf of the State. Thus, conduct of a
personal nature, which is within the definition of iure gestionis, shall not warrant
international immunity.
The sovereign immunity of States is also extended to diplomatic premises and to
diplomatic parcels, marking the property of diplomatic premises and of diplomatic
personal.
HUMAN RIGHTS IN PUBLIC INTERNATIONAL
LAW
The Universal Declaration of Human Rights of the United Nations Organization was adopted in
1948, with forty eight for, non against, and eight abstentions.
• Saudi Arabia opposed the equal treatment of sexes;
• South Africa had reason to defend the apartheid system;
• The Soviet Union, Belarus, Ukraine, Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, and Poland had communist
motivations.
The Universal Declaration of Human Rights is complemented by the International Covenant on
Civil and Political Rights, and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights.
Note however, that developed countries insist on better civil, and political rights, whereas
developing countries insist on better economic, social, and cultural rights.
